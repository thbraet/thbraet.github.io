# The Glycemic Index [DRAFT}
The Glycemic Index (GI) is a measure that ranks carbohydrate-containing foods based on how quickly and how much they raise blood glucose levels after consumption.

# How to measure GI

## Test Procedure

To measure it, subjects will first have to undergo a 12-hour fast. After this 12-hour fast they will take in a targetted food.
Be aware that only the available carbohydrate are taken into account (total carbohydrate minus the fiber content)

The procedure is undergone for a standard food, either pure glucose, which will set the benchmark of a GI of 100.
It's important that the total available carbohydrate in the test food and the benchmark food are the same!
Sometimes white bread is taken as the reference value of 100 but then glucose has a GI of about 140. Glucose is typically taken as the standard reference as there is no variablity in composition opposed to white bread.

## Calculation
After ingesting the food, you measure the blood glucose levels for a continuous period of two hours and measure the AUC (Area Under Curve)
This AUC is divided by the AUC for pure glucose which then gives you a measure between 0 and 1. Multiply this by 100 and you get your Glycemic Index.

The glycemix index is always expressed as a number between 0 and 100 using pure glucose or white bread as the reference being equal to 100


The Glycemic Index of a food can't be determined on an individual level because individual responses vary to much.
To get the GI of a food, you take a test sample of multiple people and take the average value to get the eventual GI of that food.
The result is the average glycemic response of a group of people rather than one individuel. Individual results will vary greatly

**Classification**
- _Low GI_: <55
- _Medium GI_: 56 < 69
- _High GI_: >70

Foods with carbohydrates that break down quickly during digestion and release glucose rapidly into the bloodstream tend to have a high GI; 
Foods with carbohydrates that break down more slowly, releasing glucose more gradually into the bloodstream, tend to have a low GI.

A high GI typically means that the food is digested quickly and the carbohydrates are easily released in the blood.

# The Glycemic Load
Where the Glycemic Index is a relative value, the Glycemix Load is an absolute value which you get by multiplying the Glycemix Index of that food by the total grams of carbohydrate in the serving.

# Glycemic index parameters
**Factors affecting the GI of your food**
- _Fat_: Foods containing more fat, result in a lower GI
- _Acidity_: More acid foods (e.g. by adding vinegar) result in a lower GI
- _Protein_: More protein results in a lower GI
- _Fiber_: More fiber slows down the digestion of carbohydrate thus lowering the GI
- _Ripeness_: Riper fruits will have a higher GI than less ripe fruits
- _Cooking Method_: The more cooked, or overcooked, a food, the more its cellular structure is broken, with a tendency for ti to digest quicklly and raise blood glucose more. If you let a cooked food cool down, the GI lowers again so this process is more or less reversible
- _Processing_: E.g. flour has a higher GI than whole grain as grinding breaks the grain's protective layers and the length of storage.
